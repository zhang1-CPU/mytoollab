Favicon Generator: Complete Guide for Website Icons

That tiny icon in your browser tab is more important than you think. Learn what a favicon does, which sizes you actually need in 2026, how to create one from an image, and why a good favicon generator saves you so much time.

What Is a Favicon and Why Does It Matter?

A favicon (short for "favorite icon") is the small icon that appears next to a website's title in browser tabs, bookmarks, history lists, and mobile home screens. Despite being only 16x16 pixels in its smallest form, it is one of the most recognizable elements of your brand on the web.

Think about it: when someone has 20 tabs open, how do they find yours? By the favicon. When someone scrolls through their bookmarks looking for a site they saved, the favicon is what their eye catches first. It is a tiny piece of real estate with outsized impact on brand recognition and user experience.

Quick history. Favicons were introduced by Microsoft in Internet Explorer 5 back in 1999. Originally they were just .ico files called favicon.ico in the root directory. Today, we have SVG favicons, Apple touch icons, Android icons, and more — but the original concept is the same.

Why Every Website Needs a Favicon

  • Brand recognition. A consistent favicon makes your site instantly recognizable across tabs, bookmarks, and search results.
  • Professionalism. Sites without favicons look unfinished or untrustworthy. That default globe icon in the browser tab signals "amateur hour."
  • Usability. With dozens of tabs open, users rely on favicons to navigate quickly. A distinctive icon saves them time.
  • Bookmarking. When someone bookmarks your site, the favicon appears in their bookmarks bar and menu.
  • Mobile experience. On mobile, favicons become the icon users see when they add your site to their home screen.
  • SEO signal. While not a major ranking factor, Google has shown favicons in mobile search results since 2019. A professional favicon can increase click-through rate.

Favicon Sizes You Actually Need in 2026

The days of just dropping a single favicon.ico file into your root directory are over. Today, different browsers and devices expect different sizes and formats. But you do not need 20 different files — here is the practical minimum set that covers 99% of cases:

The Essential Favicon Files

  1. favicon.ico (32x32 or multi-size) – The classic format for desktop browsers. A multi-size .ico containing 16x16, 32x32, and 48x48 covers all legacy uses.
  2. favicon.svg (vector) – The modern favorite. A single SVG file scales perfectly to any size, looks crisp on every display, and has tiny file size. Supported by all modern browsers.
  3. apple-touch-icon.png (180x180) – Used by iPhones and iPads when someone adds your site to their home screen. Apple recommends 180x180 pixels.
  4. android-chrome-192x192.png – Used by Android devices and Chrome for home screen shortcuts and PWA icons.
  5. android-chrome-512x512.png – The larger size for higher-density Android screens and the splash screen for progressive web apps.

What About All Those Other Sizes?

You will see recommendations for 16x16, 32x32, 96x96, 128x128, 152x152, 167x170, 192x192, 256x256, 512x512 — the list goes on. Here is the truth: modern browsers are very good at scaling. If you provide a high-quality 180x180 PNG and an SVG, they will handle scaling down perfectly for smaller sizes.

Do not drive yourself crazy generating 15 different files. Focus on quality over quantity.

💡
Pro tip. SVG favicons are the future. They are tiny, infinitely scalable, and even support light/dark mode via media queries. If you only have time for one modern format, make it SVG. Keep the .ico as a fallback for older browsers.

How to Create a Great Favicon

Designing a good favicon is a unique challenge because you are working with an incredibly small canvas. A logo that looks great at 500 pixels wide can turn into an unrecognizable blob at 16 pixels. Here is how to do it right.

Favicon Design Principles

  1. Simplicity first. At 16x16 pixels, detail is lost. Use simple shapes, bold lines, and high contrast. If your logo has fine text or intricate details, it will not work as a favicon — create a simplified version.
  2. Use your brand colors. Your favicon should match your brand palette so it is instantly recognizable. Use your primary brand color as the background or the icon color.
  3. Think about the shape. Circles, squares, and simple letterforms work best. Avoid thin horizontal lines (they can disappear) and very delicate details.
  4. Test at small sizes. Always check how your favicon looks at 16x16. If you cannot tell what it is, simplify it further.
  5. Consider the background. Will your favicon be on a transparent background or a colored square? Colored backgrounds usually make icons more visible against different browser themes.
  6. Keep it balanced. Leave a small amount of padding around the edge so your icon does not feel cramped. About 10-15% padding usually looks right.

Starting From an Existing Logo or Image

If you already have a logo, great — but do not just shrink it down and call it a day. You will likely need to create a simplified version specifically for the favicon:

  • If your logo is a symbol + wordmark, use just the symbol for the favicon
  • If your logo has multiple colors, try a single-color version on a solid background
  • If your logo has thin lines, thicken them so they remain visible at small sizes
  • If your logo is very detailed, extract the most recognizable element

How to Add a Favicon to Your Website

Once you have your favicon files, you need to tell browsers where to find them. Place the files in your website's root directory (or an /assets/favicons/ folder if you prefer), then add these lines inside the <head> section of every page:

  • <link rel="icon" type="image/svg+xml" href="/favicon.svg"> — modern SVG favicon
  • <link rel="icon" type="image/png" sizes="32x32" href="/favicon-32x32.png"> — PNG fallback
  • <link rel="apple-touch-icon" sizes="180x180" href="/apple-touch-icon.png"> — iOS home screen
  • <link rel="manifest" href="/site.webmanifest"> — for PWA and Android

If you put favicon.ico in your root directory, most browsers will find it automatically even without the link tag — but it is still better to include the tags explicitly.

Common Favicon Mistakes (and How to Fix Them)

1. Just Shrinking Your Full Logo

This is the #1 mistake. A logo designed for 300-pixel-wide headers will almost never look good at 16 pixels. The text becomes unreadable, details blur together, and the whole thing turns into a mushy blob. Fix: Create a simplified mark specifically for the favicon. Use just an icon, initials, or a single shape.

2. Too Much Detail

Photographs, gradients, fine lines, small text — none of these survive being shrunk to 16x16. Fix: Use bold shapes and high contrast. If you cannot identify your favicon from across the room when it is shown at 16 pixels, it is too complex.

3. Wrong Background Color

A white icon on a transparent background disappears in light-themed browsers. A dark icon gets lost in dark mode. Fix: Use a solid background color that works in both modes, or use an SVG favicon with a prefers-color-scheme media query to adapt automatically.

4. Low Resolution Source Image

If you start with a 32x32 image and try to scale it up to 512x512, it will look blurry and pixelated. Fix: Always start with the highest resolution source you have — ideally a vector SVG file, or at least a 1024x1024 PNG.

5. Forgetting the Apple Touch Icon

On iOS, if you do not provide an apple-touch-icon, Safari will just use a screenshot of your page as the home screen icon — which looks terrible. Fix: Always include a 180x180 apple-touch-icon.png. It is one file and takes 30 seconds to add.

6. Not Testing on Real Devices

Your favicon might look great on your desktop monitor but terrible on a phone. Fix: Test on actual devices — add your site to your phone's home screen and see how it looks next to your other apps.

Favicons and Branding: The Bigger Picture

Your favicon is not just a technical requirement — it is a key brand touchpoint. Think about how many times a day users see it:

  • In their browser tab while reading your content
  • In their bookmarks bar, day after day
  • In their browser history when they are looking for you again
  • On their phone's home screen, right next to their apps
  • In search results on mobile devices

Each of those is a tiny brand impression. Over time, they add up to familiarity and trust. A professional, well-designed favicon signals attention to detail and quality — even at a subconscious level.

The Future of Favicons

Favicons have come a long way since 1999, and they keep evolving. Here are some trends to watch:

SVG Favicons Becoming the Standard

SVG is increasingly the go-to format. It is a single file instead of a dozen PNGs, it is tiny (often under 1KB), it scales perfectly to any size, and it supports things like animations and dark mode detection. All major modern browsers support SVG favicons now — the holdouts are mostly older versions of Safari and IE.

Dark Mode Adaptation

With more and more users using dark mode, favicons need to work on both light and dark backgrounds. SVG favicons with @media (prefers-color-scheme: dark) media queries can change their colors automatically based on the user's theme.

Emoji Favicons

A fun trend: using emoji as favicons. They are universally understood, colorful, and instantly recognizable. You can even use SVG to create custom emoji-style icons. Just make sure it matches your brand personality.

Rich Search Results

Google has been showing favicons in mobile search results for years, and they keep experimenting with richer displays. As search becomes more visual, having a distinctive, high-quality favicon will only become more important for standing out in the SERPs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What size should a favicon be?

The most important sizes in 2026 are: 32x32 for the classic favicon.ico, 180x180 for Apple touch icons, 192x192 and 512x512 for Android, and ideally an SVG that works at any size. If you only create one, make it a high-quality 512x512 PNG and let browsers scale it down — they do a great job these days.

Can I create a favicon from an image?

Absolutely. Just upload a high-resolution PNG or JPG of your logo (at least 512x512 pixels works best) to a free favicon generator tool, and it will automatically create all the sizes and formats you need. The whole process takes less than a minute.

Do favicons affect SEO?

Favicons are not a direct Google ranking factor. However, they can indirectly affect SEO by improving click-through rate (especially on mobile, where Google shows favicons in search results) and by increasing brand recognition and trust — which lead to more clicks and return visits.

What format is best for favicons?

SVG is the best modern format — it is small, infinitely scalable, and supports features like dark mode. Keep a favicon.ico file as a fallback for older browsers, and add Apple touch and Android PNGs for mobile devices. This combination covers basically every device and browser in use today.

How do I add a favicon to my website?

Generate your favicon files, upload them to your website's root directory, then add link tags in the <head> section of your HTML pointing to the files. Most favicon generator tools will give you the exact HTML code you need to copy and paste — no coding knowledge required.

Why is my favicon not showing up?

Most common reasons: the file is not at the correct path, the link tag has a typo, the browser cache is showing an old version (try a hard refresh with Ctrl+Shift+R or Cmd+Shift+R), or the file format is wrong. Double-check the file path, clear your cache, and make sure you are using a supported format like .ico, .png, or .svg.

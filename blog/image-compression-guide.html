Image Compression Guide: JPG vs PNG vs WebP Explained

Choosing the right image format is the single biggest performance decision you make on the web. Here is how JPG, PNG, and WebP actually work — and when each one is the right tool for the job.

Lossy vs Lossless Compression: The Core Trade-Off

Every image compression discussion boils down to a single question: are you willing to discard information to save bytes, or do you need to reconstruct the original image exactly?

  • Lossy compression reduces file size by throwing away visual information that human eyes are least likely to notice — subtle color differences, high-frequency detail, and faint gradients. JPG and WebP (in lossy mode) are lossy formats.
  • Lossless compression preserves every pixel of the original. PNG and WebP (in lossless mode) are lossless formats. File sizes are larger, but no visual information is lost.
Rule of thumb. Use lossy compression for photographs and detailed artwork. Use lossless compression for logos, icons, charts, screenshots, and any image with sharp edges or transparent areas.

JPG / JPEG: The Photographic Format

JPG — Best for photographs and complex artwork

How it works: JPG splits the image into small 8×8 pixel blocks, transforms them into the frequency domain, and discards fine-grained frequency coefficients that humans are least sensitive to. The remaining information is then Huffman-encoded.

Strengths: Very small files for photos; universally supported by every browser, email client, and printer ever made; flexible quality slider from 1 to 100.

Weaknesses: No transparency support; creates ugly blocky artifacts around sharp edges, text, and flat color areas; no alpha channel; re-saving a JPG repeatedly causes visible generation loss.

Typical file sizes: A 1200×800 photograph at quality 80 is typically 100 KB to 250 KB.

PNG: The Crisp Line-Art Format

PNG — Best for screenshots, logos, charts, icons

How it works: PNG applies a per-row predictor to the pixel data, then uses lossless Deflate (ZIP) compression. It supports up to 24-bit color plus an 8-bit alpha channel, giving you fully-transparent backgrounds with smooth edges.

Strengths: Lossless quality; excellent at sharp edges and flat color; supports transparency; no generation loss when resaved; good at line-art and screenshots.

Weaknesses: Produces very large files for photographs — often 5 to 10 times bigger than a JPG equivalent; limited support for animation (only animated PNG / APNG, which is less common than GIF); slower decode than JPG in some implementations.

Typical file sizes: A 1200×800 screenshot is typically 300 KB to 1 MB. A simple logo may be only 10 KB to 50 KB.

WebP: The Modern All-Rounder

WebP — Best for modern web (both photos and UI assets)

How it works: WebP was developed by Google and released in 2010. Its lossy mode borrows ideas from video codecs: it predicts image blocks from neighboring pixels, encodes the residuals, and uses a sophisticated entropy coder. Its lossless mode uses custom transforms that typically beat PNG by 25%.

Strengths: Best-in-class compression in both lossy and lossless modes; supports alpha transparency and animation; lossy+alpha is unique to WebP and its successor AVIF; browser support is now near-universal (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari since 2020).

Weaknesses: Limited support in older tools and printers; not universally accepted by print shops; slightly slower encode than JPG at ultra-high quality settings.

Typical file sizes: A 1200×800 photograph at equivalent visual quality is typically 25% to 35% smaller than JPG; a line-art WebP is typically 20% to 40% smaller than the same PNG.

How to Choose: A Decision Tree

  1. Is the image a photograph, product shot, or complex artwork with lots of color detail? → Use WebP (lossy) on the modern web. Fall back to JPG if you must support very old browsers or print shops.
  2. Is the image a logo, icon, screenshot, chart, or anything with text or sharp edges? → Use WebP (lossless) for web. Keep a PNG version for use in documents, email clients, and print.
  3. Do you need transparency and a small file size at the same time? → WebP is the clear winner — it supports both lossy compression and transparency together.
  4. Will the image be printed, placed in a word processor, or forwarded as an email attachment? → Stick with JPG or PNG. Not every tool chain knows how to handle WebP yet.
Pro tip for web teams. Offer WebP with a JPG/PNG fallback in an HTML <picture> element. Modern browsers get the small WebP; older browsers still work. You save 30% of bytes for most visitors without sacrificing quality.

Understanding Quality Settings

Most compression tools expose a quality slider (often 1–100). Higher numbers preserve more detail at the cost of larger files. Here is how to think about them:

  • Quality 90–100 (preservation): almost visually identical to the original. Use for master copies and professional photography. Files are large.
  • Quality 80–85 (recommended web default): invisible-to-nearly-invisible quality loss, roughly 50% of the original file size. Great default for web images.
  • Quality 60–75 (aggressive): small files, some softening visible on close inspection. Acceptable for thumbnail galleries and hero images viewed at normal size.
  • Quality below 50 (avoid): visible blocking and ringing artifacts around edges. Only appropriate for very small thumbnails that will never be viewed large.

Step-by-Step Image Compression Workflow

  1. Resize first. If the original is 4000 pixels wide but your content area is only 1200 pixels wide, resize down before compressing. Extra pixels wasted bytes that nobody will see.
  2. Choose the right format. Photograph → WebP or JPG. Line-art → WebP or PNG.
  3. Try quality 80 first. Inspect visually at 100% zoom.
  4. Lower quality until you see artifacts, then back off. The lowest quality that still looks good is your sweet spot.
  5. Strip metadata. EXIF data, GPS coordinates, and color profiles are often unnecessary for web display and can add tens of kilobytes.
  6. Test on real devices. Compression artifacts look different on mobile screens with higher pixel density — always spot-check on a phone.

Pro Tips for Even Smaller Files

  • Use progressive JPG or interlaced PNG. Both formats render a low-resolution preview while the full image downloads, which feels faster to users.
  • Serve responsive images. Use HTML srcset / sizes so mobile users get appropriately-sized files, not your 2000-pixel hero image.
  • Avoid re-encoding lossy formats. Every time you open and re-save a JPG, more detail is lost. Always keep an uncompressed or lossless master and derive web versions from it.
  • Use lazy loading. Modern browsers support loading="lazy" on <img> tags — defer offscreen images to speed up initial page load.
  • Put images through a CDN. Content delivery networks often auto-generate modern formats (WebP, AVIF) and apply lossless optimization automatically.

Common Image Compression Mistakes

  • Saving a photograph as PNG. A 1200-pixel photo saved as PNG can be 2 MB when a good JPG would be 100 KB.
  • Using JPG for logos and icons. JPG introduces ugly halos around sharp edges and text on flat-colored backgrounds.
  • Uploading the raw camera file. A 6000×4000 12 MB JPG is overkill for every web use case — resize before publishing.
  • Ignoring transparency. A PNG with a large alpha channel can be bigger than it needs to be; WebP lossy+alpha often halves the file size while looking identical.
  • Not inspecting output. Always zoom in and look at the image at 100% size. What looked fine at 50% zoom can be visibly compressed at native size.

Types of QR Codes: Which One Should You Use?

QR codes are everywhere, but not all QR codes are the same. Some open websites, others A free QR code types tool is essential — add contacts, connect to WiFi, send text messages, or even make phone calls. Choosing the right type of QR code for your use case makes the scanning experience smoother and more effective.

How QR Codes Work (Quick Overview)

Before diving into the types, let us quickly cover how QR codes work. QR (Quick Response) codes are two-dimensional barcodes that store data as a pattern of black and white squares. When you scan one with your phone camera, the camera reads the pattern and decodes the data inside.

The data inside a QR code is structured. The code includes information about what type of data it contains — a URL, plain text, contact information, etc. Your phone's camera or QR scanner app recognizes the type and takes the appropriate action: opening a browser, adding a contact, connecting to WiFi, and so on.

This is why different types matter. A URL QR code is designed to open a web page, and scanners know to launch a browser. A vCard QR code contains contact information, and scanners know to offer to add it to your address book. Using the right type gives users a seamless, one-tap experience.

12+ Types of QR Codes Explained

Here are the most common and useful QR code types, what they do, and when to use each one.

1. URL QR Code

The most common type. When scanned, it opens a web page in the user's browser. This is the go-to type for most marketing use cases.

Use it for: Linking to your website, a landing page, a product page, a menu, a Google Form, a YouTube video — basically anything that lives on the web.

Best practice: Use a short, clean URL. Long URLs create denser QR codes that are harder to scan. If you have a long URL, use a URL shortener first.

2. Text QR Code

Stores plain text. When scanned, it just displays the text on the user's screen — no automatic action.

Use it for: Simple information display, instructions, WiFi passwords (though there is a better type for that), secret messages, or any situation where you just want to show text without linking anywhere.

Limitations: Text is just text — no links, no actions. Users can copy the text but have to manually act on it.

3. vCard / Contact QR Code

Stores contact information in vCard format: name, phone number, email, address, company, job title, website, etc. When scanned, the user's phone offers to add the contact to their address book with one tap.

Common fields in a vCard QR code:

  • Name and job title
  • Phone numbers (work, mobile, fax)
  • Email addresses
  • Company and address
  • Website and social media links
  • Notes and other custom fields

Use it for: Business cards, conference badges, email signatures, networking events, "contact us" signage.

Why it is great: No typing required. Scanning a vCard and tapping "Save Contact" is much faster and more reliable than manually entering contact info.

4. WiFi QR Code

Stores WiFi network credentials (network name, password, and encryption type). When scanned, the user's phone can connect to the WiFi network automatically — no typing required.

Use it for: Cafes, restaurants, co-working spaces, hotels, waiting rooms, your home, any place where you want to make WiFi access easy.

Why it is great: No more "what is the WiFi password?" questions a hundred times a day. And no more mistyping long, complicated passwords. It also means you can use a strong WiFi password without making it hard for guests to connect.

5. SMS QR Code

Pre-fills a text message with a recipient phone number and optional message body. When scanned, it opens the user's texting app with everything ready — they just tap "send."

Use it for: SMS marketing campaigns, text-to-vote, text-to-win contests, customer support by text, quick contact options.

Example: A real estate sign with a QR code that texts "INFO" to a number to get property details.

6. Email QR Code

Pre-fills an email with a recipient address, subject line, and optional body text. When scanned, it opens the user's email app with everything filled in — they just tap "send."

Use it for: Contact information, customer support emails, newsletter signups, feedback collection, lead generation.

Why it is great: Removes the friction of typing an email address and subject line. Users are much more likely to send a quick email if everything is already filled in.

7. Phone / Call QR Code

Stores a phone number. When scanned, it opens the phone dialer with the number pre-filled — the user just taps "call."

Use it for: Business cards, "call now" signage, customer support, emergency contact numbers, appointment booking by phone.

Why it is great: No dialing required. Especially useful on mobile — where people are already on their phone and can tap to call instantly.

8. Location / Geo QR Code

Stores latitude and longitude coordinates. When scanned, it opens the user's maps app at that location, often with navigation options.

Use it for: Physical store locations, event venues, pop-up shops, real estate listings, tourist information points, "find us" signage.

Why it is great: No searching for an address or typing it into maps. Just scan and get directions. Perfect for businesses that are hard to find.

9. Event / Calendar QR Code

Stores event details in vCalendar or iCal format: event title, start and end time, location, description, etc. When scanned, it offers to add the event to the user's calendar.

Use it for: Concerts, conferences, workshops, webinars, meetings, appointments, any event where you want people to add it to their calendar.

Why it is great: No manually entering event details. One scan and the event is on their calendar with a reminder set. This dramatically increases attendance rates.

10. Social Media QR Code

Links directly to a social media profile or page. When scanned, it opens the profile in the app (if installed) or in the browser.

Use it for: Growing your social media following, cross-promoting your social accounts, putting on packaging or store signage, "follow us" campaigns.

Platforms: Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, Twitter/X, LinkedIn, YouTube, Snapchat, and more. Each platform has its own QR code style, but you can also just use a URL QR code that links to your profile.

11. Payment QR Code

Links to a payment page or contains payment information. When scanned, it takes the user to a checkout page or opens a payment app.

Use it for: In-store payments, restaurant table ordering and payment, invoices and bills, donations, tipping, street vendors, market stalls.

Examples: PayPal QR codes, Venmo QR codes, UPI QR codes (popular in India), Alipay and WeChat Pay (popular in China), Stripe payment links.

12. PDF / File QR Code

Links to a file (PDF, image, document, etc.) hosted online. When scanned, it opens or downloads the file.

Use it for: Digital menus, product manuals, brochures, catalogs, whitepapers, event programs, any document you want people to have access to without printing.

Why it is great: Instead of printing hundreds of physical documents, put a QR code on one sign and let people download the digital version. It is cheaper, more environmentally friendly, and you can update the file anytime.

Static vs. Dynamic QR Codes: What Is the Difference?

Beyond the data type, there is another important distinction: static vs. dynamic QR codes.

Static QR Codes

In a static QR code, the data is encoded directly into the pattern of squares. The link or text is built into the code itself.

Pros:

  • Free to create — no subscription needed
  • Work forever (no expiration, no service dependency)
  • Simple and reliable
  • No data tracking (if privacy is a concern)

Cons:

  • Cannot be changed once created
  • No analytics — you cannot see how many times it was scanned
  • Denser for complex data (like long URLs or vCards)

Dynamic QR Codes

A dynamic QR code contains a short URL that redirects to your actual destination. Because the redirect happens on a server, you can change the target URL anytime — even after you have printed the code.

Pros:

  • Editable — change the destination anytime
  • Analytics — track scans, dates, times, locations
  • Smaller/denser code (short URL = simpler pattern)
  • A/B testing — send different people to different pages

Cons:

  • Usually requires a paid subscription
  • Depends on the service — if they go out of business, your code stops working
  • The redirect adds a tiny bit of latency
Which one should you use?
• Use static for permanent, simple things: WiFi codes, personal vCards, your main website URL
• Use dynamic for marketing campaigns, anything temporary, and whenever you want analytics
• When in doubt, start with dynamic — the analytics are worth it for business use

How to Choose the Right QR Code Type

With so many types, how do you pick the right one? Ask yourself these questions:

  1. What action do I want people to take? If you want them to visit a website, use URL. If you want them to call you, use phone. If you want them to add a contact, use vCard. Match the type to the action.
  2. Will the destination change? If you might need to update it later, use a dynamic QR code.
  3. Do I need to track scans? If you want analytics, use dynamic. Static codes cannot be tracked.
  4. How much data am I storing? More data = denser code = harder to scan. Keep it simple — use the minimum data needed for the action.
  5. Where will the code be scanned? If it will be scanned from far away (billboard, poster), use a simple type with minimal data so the code is less dense and easier to scan at a distance.

For most business and marketing uses, a URL QR code (dynamic, with analytics) is the most versatile choice. It links to a web page where you can include any information or action you want, and you can update the page anytime.

FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

How many types of QR codes are there?

There are dozens of QR code data types, but only about a dozen are commonly used. The most popular types are URL, vCard, WiFi, SMS, email, phone, location, event, social media, payment, and plain text. Most QR code generators support at least these 10–12 common types.

What is the most common type of QR code?

URL QR codes are by far the most common. Most QR codes you see in the wild — on posters, packaging, menus, advertisements — are URL codes that link to a website or landing page.

Can I change what a QR code links to after I print it?

Only if it is a dynamic QR code. Dynamic codes use a redirect URL that can be updated anytime. Static QR codes have the data encoded directly into them and cannot be changed once printed. Always use dynamic codes for marketing materials that you might want to update later.

Do all phones support all QR code types?

Most modern smartphones (iOS 11+ and most Android devices) can natively scan and recognize the most common QR code types: URL, vCard, WiFi, SMS, email, phone, and calendar events. More obscure types may require a dedicated QR scanner app. For maximum compatibility, stick to the well-known types.

What is the difference between a QR code and a barcode?

Traditional barcodes are one-dimensional (they store data in vertical lines) and hold very little data — usually just a product number. QR codes are two-dimensional (they store data in a grid of squares) and can hold much more data: URLs, text, contact info, etc. QR codes also have built-in error correction, so they still work even if part of the code is damaged or covered.

How much data can a QR code hold?

It depends on the QR code version (size) and error correction level. The largest QR codes can hold several kilobytes of data — thousands of characters of text. But more data means a denser, harder-to-scan code. For best scannability, keep the data under 200–300 characters whenever possible.

Can I create custom QR codes with my logo?

Yes, most QR code generators let you add a logo in the center and customize colors. Just be careful not to cover too much of the code — QR codes have error correction, but only up to a point.

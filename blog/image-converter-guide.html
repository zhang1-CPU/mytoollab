Image Converter: JPG, PNG, WebP – Which Format to Choose

JPG, PNG, WebP, GIF, SVG — the list of image formats goes on. Each one has strengths and weaknesses. This guide explains which format to use for what, why it matters for page speed, and how to convert images online in seconds.

Why Image Format Matters More Than You Think

Pick the wrong image format and you could be costing yourself money, users, and search rankings. Images are typically the largest files on a webpage, and their size directly affects how fast your page loads. Faster pages rank better in Google, have lower bounce rates, and convert more visitors into customers.

But it is not just about file size. Different formats handle different kinds of images differently. A format that is perfect for a photograph can be terrible for a logo, and vice versa. Using the right format in the right situation gives you the best possible quality at the smallest possible file size.

Quick fact. Images make up about 75% of the total weight of an average webpage. Just converting images from JPG or PNG to WebP can reduce their size by 25-35% with no visible loss in quality. That is a huge difference in page load time.

Lossy vs. Lossless Compression

Before we dive into individual formats, there is one key concept you need to understand: the difference between lossy and lossless compression.

  • Lossy compression. Permanently removes some image data to make the file smaller. The algorithm discards details that the human eye is least likely to notice. Smaller file sizes, but quality degrades if you compress too much. JPG and WebP (lossy mode) are lossy formats.
  • Lossless compression. Compresses the image without losing any data. The image looks exactly the same — the file is just stored more efficiently. Larger file sizes than lossy, but perfect quality. PNG and WebP (lossless mode) are lossless formats.

JPG (JPEG): Best for Photographs

JPG (or JPEG, short for Joint Photographic Experts Group) is the most common image format on the web. It was created specifically for photographs and realistic images with smooth gradients and lots of colors.

What JPG Is Good At

  • Photographs. JPG was literally designed for photos. It handles millions of colors and subtle gradients beautifully.
  • Small file sizes. At reasonable quality levels, JPG produces very small files for photographic content.
  • Universal support. Every browser, device, app, and printer supports JPG. It is the most compatible format there is.
  • Adjustable quality. You can choose exactly how much compression to apply, trading file size for quality.

What JPG Is Bad At

  • Text and sharp edges. JPG compression creates blurry artifacts around text, lines, and sharp edges.
  • Transparency. JPG does not support transparent backgrounds at all.
  • Screenshots and graphics. Anything with flat colors, hard edges, or text looks worse as a JPG.
  • Repeated editing. Every time you save a JPG, you lose a little more quality. Edit from the original, not from a previously compressed JPG.
📷
Use JPG for: product photos, blog post hero images, profile pictures, travel photos, food photography — basically any photographic content. If it looks like something you would take with a camera, JPG is probably the right choice.

PNG: Best for Graphics with Transparency

PNG (Portable Network Graphics) is a lossless format designed for web graphics. It was created as an improved replacement for GIF, and it is the go-to format for anything that needs transparency or has sharp edges and flat colors.

What PNG Is Good At

  • Transparency. PNG supports alpha transparency, meaning you can have images with see-through backgrounds. Essential for logos, icons, and overlays.
  • Text and sharp edges. PNG preserves crisp lines and text perfectly — no blurry compression artifacts.
  • Flat colors. Great for graphics with solid color areas like logos, icons, infographics, and screenshots.
  • Lossless quality. You can save and re-save a PNG as many times as you want and it never loses quality.

What PNG Is Bad At

  • Photographs. PNG file sizes for photos are enormous — often 5-10 times larger than an equivalent-quality JPG. Never use PNG for photos unless you have a very specific reason.
  • Large file sizes. Even for graphics, PNG files can get big quickly if there are lots of colors or details.
  • No animation. Unlike GIF, PNG does not support animation (though there is APNG, which is not universally supported).

WebP: The Modern All-Rounder

WebP (pronounced "weppy") is a modern image format developed by Google. It is unique because it supports both lossy and lossless compression, and it consistently produces smaller files than both JPG and PNG at the same quality level.

Why WebP Is Better

  • Smaller files. Lossy WebP is typically 25-35% smaller than JPG at the same visual quality. Lossless WebP is about 25% smaller than PNG.
  • Transparency support. WebP supports alpha transparency like PNG, but in a much smaller file. A transparent WebP is often smaller than both a PNG and a JPG.
  • Animation support. Animated WebP files are smaller than animated GIFs with better quality.
  • Great for both photos and graphics. WebP handles photographic content and flat graphics well, making it a versatile choice.

WebP Drawbacks

  • Browser support. While all modern browsers support WebP (Chrome, Firefox, Safari 14+, Edge), very old browsers do not. If you need to support IE or really old Safari versions, you will need fallbacks.
  • Less universal. Some apps and tools still do not support WebP natively. You might need to convert to JPG or PNG for certain use cases.
  • Lossy editing. Like JPG, lossy WebP loses quality each time you re-save it. Keep an original master file.
Recommendation for 2026. Use WebP as your primary format for website images. It is supported by over 97% of users globally and provides the best balance of quality and file size. Serve JPG or PNG fallbacks only if you absolutely must support ancient browsers.

Other Formats You Might Encounter

GIF

GIF is the classic animated image format. It only supports 256 colors, which makes it terrible for photographs but fine for simple animations. For short clips, animated WebP or even video (MP4) usually gives better quality at smaller file sizes. GIF is mostly kept around for meme culture and broad compatibility.

SVG

SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics) is not like the other formats. Instead of storing pixels, it stores vector shapes and paths as code. This means SVGs are infinitely scalable — they look crisp at any size, from 16px to 1600px. They also have tiny file sizes for simple graphics. Best for logos, icons, and simple illustrations.

AVIF

AVIF is a newer format based on the AV1 video codec. It can produce even smaller files than WebP, especially at very high compression levels. Support is growing but not as universal as WebP yet. Worth keeping an eye on.

When to Convert Between Formats

Here are the most common scenarios where you would need to use an image converter:

JPG to PNG

Rarely a good idea. If you have a JPG photo and convert it to PNG, you get all the compression artifacts from the JPG but in a much bigger file. PNG cannot magically restore lost quality. Only convert JPG to PNG if you need to add transparency or edit the image and want to avoid further quality loss during editing.

PNG to JPG

Common for screenshots or graphics that accidentally got saved as PNG but would be smaller as JPG. If you have a photographic image saved as PNG, converting to JPG will dramatically reduce the file size. Just make sure you do not need transparency.

JPG or PNG to WebP

This is the conversion that everyone should be doing for their website. Converting JPG and PNG images to WebP is one of the quickest wins for page speed. Smaller files, same quality, better performance. Just make sure to keep your original files as well.

WebP to JPG or PNG

Sometimes you need a WebP image in a more compatible format. Maybe you want to attach it to an email, upload it to a tool that does not support WebP, or edit it in an app that only works with JPG/PNG. Converting back is easy with a good image converter.

Image Format and SEO: The Connection

Choosing the right image format is not just about aesthetics — it directly impacts your SEO. Here is how:

Page Speed

Google uses page speed as a ranking factor, and images are usually the biggest contributor to page weight. Using modern formats like WebP and properly compressing your images makes your pages load faster, which helps you rank higher.

Image Search

Google Images drives a ton of traffic. Optimizing your images — including using the right format — helps them rank better in image search results, which can bring in a whole separate stream of visitors.

User Experience

Faster pages have lower bounce rates and higher engagement. When images load quickly, users stay on your site longer, view more pages, and are more likely to convert. All of these are positive signals to Google.

Core Web Vitals

Google's Core Web Vitals metrics — especially Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) — are heavily influenced by images. Optimizing your image formats and sizes is one of the most impactful things you can do to improve your Core Web Vitals scores.

Best Practices for Image Optimization

Format choice is just one piece of the image optimization puzzle. Here is the full checklist:

  1. Choose the right format. WebP for most things on websites, JPG for photos if you need maximum compatibility, PNG for when you need lossless quality and transparency.
  2. Resize before you compress. Never upload a 4000-pixel-wide image to display at 800 pixels wide. Resize it first, then compress.
  3. Compress smartly. Use the highest quality level that still produces an acceptable file size. Do not over-compress to the point where the image looks bad.
  4. Use descriptive filenames. Name your images something meaningful (not IMG_1234.jpg). It helps with image SEO.
  5. Write good alt text. Alt text describes images for screen readers and search engines. Important for both accessibility and SEO.
  6. Lazy load offscreen images. Load images only when they are about to come into view. Most browsers support native lazy loading now.
  7. Serve responsive images. Use srcset to serve different sizes to different screen sizes. Mobile users do not need desktop-sized images.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which image format is highest quality?

Quality depends on what you mean. For pixel-perfect lossless quality, PNG or lossless WebP are best — every pixel is preserved exactly. For photographs, a high-quality JPG or lossy WebP is visually indistinguishable from the original to most people, at a fraction of the file size. If you are editing or archiving, use lossless. For web display, lossy at high quality is usually the best balance.

How do I convert JPG to PNG or WebP?

The easiest way is to use a free image converter tool online. Just drag and drop your JPG, select the output format you want (PNG or WebP), and download the converted file. No software installation, no account creation, completely free.

Is WebP better than JPG and PNG?

For web use, yes — WebP is generally better than both. Lossy WebP is smaller than JPG at the same quality, and lossless WebP is smaller than PNG. It also supports transparency and animation. The only reason not to use WebP is if you need to support very old browsers (which are rare in 2026) or if you are using a tool that does not support the format.

Does converting from JPG to PNG improve quality?

No. Converting a JPG to PNG cannot bring back quality that was already lost to JPG compression. You will get all the same compression artifacts but in a larger file. PNG is a lossless format, but it cannot create detail that is not there. Always keep your original uncompressed master file if you might need different formats later.

Can I convert images online for free?

Absolutely. There are many free online image converters available. Look for one that supports the formats you need (JPG, PNG, WebP at minimum), processes images in your browser for privacy, and does not require signup. What is the best image format for a website?

In 2026, WebP is the best overall format for websites. It has excellent browser support (over 97% of users), produces the smallest files, and works for both photos and graphics. For maximum compatibility, you can serve WebP as the primary format with a JPG/PNG fallback. For logos and icons that need to be scalable, SVG is unbeatable.

Color Picker Guide: Choosing Colors for Web Design

Color is one of the most powerful tools in web design. It sets mood, guides attention, communicates brand identity, and affects accessibility. But choosing the right colors — and converting between hex, RGB, and HSL — can be challenging. This guide covers everything you need to know about picking colors for the web.

Understanding Web Color Formats

Colors on the web can be described in several different formats. Each has its own strengths and use cases. A good color picker tool will let you switch between them effortlessly.

HEX (Hexadecimal)

HEX colors are the most common format on the web. They use a six-digit hexadecimal number preceded by a hash symbol, representing red, green, and blue channels (RRGGBB). Each channel goes from 00 (no color) to FF (full intensity).

#2563eb  /* brand blue */
#ff0000  /* pure red */
#ffffff  /* white */
#000000  /* black */
#ffffff80 /* white with 50% opacity (8-digit hex) */

HEX is compact, widely supported, and the default format for most design tools. Its main downside is that values are not very human-readable — it is hard to look at a HEX code and immediately know what color it is.

RGB and RGBA

RGB colors represent red, green, and blue as numbers from 0 to 255. RGBA adds a fourth value — alpha — for opacity, ranging from 0 (fully transparent) to 1 (fully opaque).

rgb(37, 99, 235)      /* brand blue */
rgba(37, 99, 235, 0.5) /* 50% opaque blue */
rgb(255, 0, 0)         /* pure red */

RGB is more human-readable than HEX because you can see the individual channel values. RGBA is essential when you need transparent colors for overlays, shadows, or glass-morphism effects.

HSL and HSLA

HSL stands for Hue, Saturation, and Lightness. This color model is often considered the most intuitive for humans to work with:

  • Hue: The color on the color wheel, from 0 to 360 degrees (0 = red, 120 = green, 240 = blue).
  • Saturation: How vibrant the color is, from 0% (gray) to 100% (full color).
  • Lightness: How light or dark the color is, from 0% (black) to 100% (white).
hsl(221, 83%, 53%)  /* brand blue */
hsl(0, 100%, 50%)   /* pure red */
hsl(221, 83%, 70%)  /* lighter version of brand blue */

HSL is fantastic for creating color schemes because you can easily make lighter or darker versions of a color by adjusting just the lightness value, or create harmonious colors by shifting the hue.

Color Theory Basics for Web Design

You do not need a degree in fine art to choose good colors for the web. Understanding a few basic concepts will take you a long way:

The Color Wheel

The color wheel is a circular arrangement of colors organized by hue. It is the foundation of color theory. Key relationships include:

  • Complementary: Colors opposite each other on the wheel (like blue and orange). They create high contrast and visual interest.
  • Analogous: Colors next to each other on the wheel (like blue, teal, and green). They create harmonious, low-contrast palettes.
  • Triadic: Three colors evenly spaced around the wheel. They provide balanced contrast while remaining harmonious.

Color Psychology

Different colors evoke different emotional responses. While cultural and personal variations exist, these general associations hold for most Western audiences:

  • Blue: Trust, professionalism, calm, technology. Used by banks, tech companies, and healthcare.
  • Red: Energy, urgency, passion, danger. Used for calls to action, sales, and alerts.
  • Green: Nature, growth, health, money. Used for environmental brands, finance, and wellness.
  • Purple: Luxury, creativity, royalty. Used by premium brands and creative companies.
  • Orange: Friendliness, enthusiasm, affordability. Used by value brands and food companies.

Value and Contrast

Value (lightness or darkness) is often more important than hue for creating clear, readable interfaces. The difference in value between text and background — contrast — determines how readable your content is. Aim for high contrast for body text and important UI elements.

Building a Web Color Palette

A good web design color palette typically includes these key colors:

Primary Color

Your main brand color — the one people associate with your product. It is used for buttons, links, logos, and key UI elements. Most sites use blue as their primary color because it conveys trust and is easy on the eyes.

Secondary Colors (Accents)

One or two accent colors used to highlight important elements, draw attention to calls to action, and add visual variety. Good accent colors complement the primary color without clashing.

Neutral Colors

Shades of white, gray, and black form the foundation of your interface. They are used for text, backgrounds, borders, and most UI elements. A good set of neutrals — from near-white to near-black with several steps in between — is essential for a polished design.

Semantic Colors

Colors that communicate meaning: green for success, red for error, yellow for warning, blue for information. These are used for alerts, form validation, status indicators, and notifications.

Start with 6-8 colors. You do not need dozens of colors. A primary, a secondary, 4-6 neutral shades, and a few semantic colors are usually enough. You can always add more tints and shades later.

Color Accessibility: A Non-Negotiable

Beautiful colors mean nothing if people cannot read your content. Color accessibility is both an ethical requirement and often a legal one. Here is what you need to know:

Contrast Ratios

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) define minimum contrast ratios for text:

  • AA standard: 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text (18pt+ or 14pt+ bold)
  • AAA standard: 7:1 for normal text, 4.5:1 for large text

Always check the contrast ratio between your text color and background color. An RGB color picker that shows contrast ratios makes this easy.

Do Not Rely on Color Alone

About 8% of men have some form of color vision deficiency. Never use color as the only way to convey information. Always pair color with icons, text labels, or patterns. For example, a success message should have both a green color and a checkmark icon.

Test in Grayscale

A quick way to check if your design works without color is to view it in grayscale. If the layout is clear and the information hierarchy is obvious in black and white, your color choices are supporting the design rather than carrying it.

Common Color Mistakes to Avoid

  • Too many colors. Using more than 3-4 major colors in an interface creates visual chaos. Restraint is key.
  • Poor contrast. Light gray text on a white background might look "clean" but it is unreadable for many people. Always test contrast.
  • Clashing hues. Colors that fight each other for attention are tiring to look at. Use a color harmony system (complementary, analogous, etc.) to guide your choices.
  • Overusing saturated colors. Highly saturated colors are great for buttons and accents but overwhelming in large areas. Use desaturated colors for backgrounds and large surfaces.
  • Ignoring dark mode. Colors that look great on white often do not work well on dark backgrounds. Test your palette in both light and dark modes.
  • Being inconsistent. Use the same blue for all links, the same green for all success states. Inconsistent color usage confuses users and makes your product feel unpolished.

Color Workflow Tips for Web Designers

  1. Define your palette early. Establish your color system at the beginning of a project and use it consistently. It is much harder to fix color issues late in the process.
  2. Use CSS variables. Define your colors as CSS custom properties so you can change them in one place. This is essential for supporting dark mode and maintaining consistency.
  3. Create a scale of neutrals. Do not just pick one gray. Create a scale of 8-10 steps from white to black so you always have the right shade for borders, backgrounds, secondary text, and more.
  4. Steal like an artist. Look at well-designed websites and apps you admire. Use a hex color picker browser extension to sample colors and see what works. Then adapt what you learn to your own project.
  5. Iterate. Your first color choice is rarely your best. Try several variations, test them in context, and refine. Small adjustments to saturation or lightness can make a huge difference.
  6. Use a color converter. When working between design tools and code, you often need the same color in different formats. A good online color converter saves time and prevents typos.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between HEX, RGB, and HSL?

All three represent the same colors but in different ways. HEX uses six hexadecimal digits (#RRGGBB), RGB uses three numbers from 0-255 for red, green, and blue, and HSL uses hue (0-360 degrees), saturation (0-100%), and lightness (0-100%). HEX is the most common on the web, RGB is good for transparency (RGBA), and HSL is the most intuitive for creating color variations.

How do I convert hex to RGB?

You can convert manually by splitting the hex code into red, green, and blue pairs and converting each pair from hexadecimal to decimal. But it is much easier to use a color converter tool — just paste your hex code and get the RGB values instantly. zlbox's color picker does this conversion automatically in real time.

What makes a good website color palette?

A good web color palette has a clear primary color, one or two accent colors, a range of neutral grays, and semantic colors for status states. Most importantly, it has sufficient contrast for accessibility, creates a clear visual hierarchy, and supports the brand and purpose of the website without overwhelming the content.

How many colors should I use on my website?

For most websites, 1-2 primary/secondary colors plus 5-8 neutral shades plus 3-4 semantic colors (success, warning, error, info) is plenty. The actual total number of color values will be higher when you include tints, shades, and hover states, but the number of distinct hues should be small.

What is the WCAG contrast ratio requirement?

WCAG 2.1 AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (18pt or larger, or 14pt or larger if bold). The AAA standard requires 7:1 and 4.5:1 respectively. Always test your text and background colors with a contrast checker before shipping.

How do I choose a primary color for my brand?

Start by thinking about the personality you want to convey and the associations different colors have. Consider your industry and competitors — you may want to fit in or stand out. Test several options, create full palettes from each, and see which one works best across different use cases (buttons, backgrounds, icons, etc.).

Everyday Percentages: How to Calculate Discounts, Tips, and Tax

Percentages show up everywhere in daily life: sale prices at the store, restaurant tips, sales tax, pay raises, interest rates. You could just pull out your calculator every time, but it is useful to understand how the math works. This guide covers all the common percentage calculations you encounter in everyday life — and how to do them quickly.

The Basics: What Is a Percentage?

Percentage means "per hundred." It is a way of expressing a number as a fraction of 100. 25% means 25 out of 100, or 0.25 as a decimal, or 1/4 as a fraction.

The basic formula for calculating a percentage of something is:

The basic percentage formula.
Amount = Original number × Percentage ÷ 100
Or more simply: convert the percentage to a decimal (divide by 100) and multiply.

For example: 20% of $80. 20% as a decimal is 0.20. 80 × 0.20 = 16. So 20% of $80 is $16.

That is the core idea. Everything else — discounts, tips, tax, increases, decreases — is just variations on this basic calculation.

Calculating Discounts: How Much Will You Save?

Sales and discounts are everywhere: "30% off everything," "extra 15% at checkout," "buy one get one 50% off." Here is how to calculate them quickly.

Simple Percentage Discount

If something costs $60 and it is 25% off, how much do you pay?

Method 1: Calculate the discount, then subtract.

  1. Discount amount = $60 × 0.25 = $15
  2. Final price = $60 - $15 = $45

Method 2: Calculate what percentage you pay, then multiply.

  1. If it is 25% off, you pay 75% (100% - 25%)
  2. Final price = $60 × 0.75 = $45

Method 2 is often faster, especially if you just want the final price and do not care about the exact discount amount.

Stacked Discounts

What if you have multiple discounts — like "20% off, plus an extra 10% at checkout"? They do not just add up to 30%. The second discount applies to the already-discounted price.

Example: $100 item, 20% off, then extra 10% off.

  1. After first discount: $100 × 0.80 = $80
  2. After second discount: $80 × 0.90 = $72
  3. Total discount: $28 (not $30)

Two discounts of 20% and 10% equal a total discount of 28%, not 30%. Always apply them sequentially, not together.

Calculating Tips: How Much to Leave

Tipping is one of the most common percentage calculations people do in their heads. Here are the standard tipping rates and how to calculate them quickly.

Standard Tipping Rates

  • 15%: Basic, average service
  • 18%: Good service (common baseline in many places)
  • 20%: Great service
  • 25%+: Exceptional service

Quick Tip Calculation Tricks

You do not need a calculator for these. Use these mental math shortcuts:

10% is easy: Just move the decimal point one place left. For a $48 bill, 10% is $4.80.

20% is double the 10% amount: $4.80 × 2 = $9.60.

15% is 10% plus half of 10%: $4.80 + $2.40 = $7.20.

18% is 20% minus 2% (which is 10% ÷ 5): $9.60 - $0.96 = $8.64.

Quick tip trick. If you want to tip roughly 20%, just double the tax. In places with around 8–10% sales tax, doubling the tax gives you about 16–20% — close enough for a quick tip.

Tip on Pre-Tax or Post-Tax Amount?

This is a common question. Etiquette-wise, tipping on the pre-tax amount is standard — the tax goes to the government, not the server. But many people tip on the total (including tax) because it is easier and adds a little extra. Either is fine; just be consistent.

Splitting the Bill

Calculating your share of a group dinner? Calculate the tip first, then add tax, then divide by the number of people. Or calculate what each person owes individually and add their share of the tip.

Sales Tax and VAT: Calculating the Total

Sales tax (or VAT/GST in many countries) adds a percentage to the price of goods and services. The rate varies by location — from 0% in some places to 20%+ in others.

How to Calculate Sales Tax

If an item costs $50 and the sales tax rate is 8%, how much do you pay total?

Method:

  1. Tax amount = $50 × 0.08 = $4.00
  2. Total price = $50 + $4 = $54.00

Or faster: Multiply by (1 + tax rate). $50 × 1.08 = $54.00

Working Backward: Price Before Tax

Sometimes you know the total price and the tax rate and want to find the original price before tax. For example, if you paid $108 total and the tax rate is 8%, what was the pre-tax price?

The formula: Pre-tax price = Total price ÷ (1 + tax rate)

$108 ÷ 1.08 = $100. The original price was $100, and tax was $8.

VAT (Value-Added Tax)

VAT works similarly to sales tax, but it is usually included in the displayed price in many countries. If a price includes VAT and you need to find the pre-VAT price, use the same backward-calculation formula.

For example: a shirt costs €59 including 20% VAT. What is the price before VAT?

€59 ÷ 1.20 = €49.17 pre-VAT, with €9.83 of VAT.

Percentage Increase and Decrease

Sometimes you want to know by what percentage something has gone up or down. "My rent went from $1,200 to $1,350 — what percentage increase is that?" or "Stock was $50, now it is $40 — what percentage drop?"

Percentage Increase

Percentage increase = (New value - Old value) ÷ Old value × 100

Example: Rent goes from $1,200 to $1,350.

  1. Increase = $1,350 - $1,200 = $150
  2. Percentage increase = $150 ÷ $1,200 = 0.125 = 12.5%

Percentage Decrease

Percentage decrease = (Old value - New value) ÷ Old value × 100

Example: Stock goes from $50 to $40.

  1. Decrease = $50 - $40 = $10
  2. Percentage decrease = $10 ÷ $50 = 0.20 = 20%
Important: You always divide by the original (old) value, not the new one. This trips a lot of people up. The percentage change is always relative to the starting point.

The "Reverse Percentage" Trap

Here is a common gotcha: if something goes up by 25% and then goes down by 25%, you do not end up where you started.

Example: $100 goes up 25% to $125. Then it goes down 25%. 25% of $125 is $31.25, so it goes down to $93.75 — less than the original $100.

Why? Because the 25% decrease is calculated from the higher number. This is why the stock market: a 20% drop requires a 25% gain to get back to even, and a 50% drop requires a 100% gain.

Simple Interest and Compound Interest

Interest rates are percentages too. You encounter them in savings accounts, loans, credit cards, and mortgages.

Simple Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal amount.

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Example: $1,000 at 5% annual simple interest for 3 years.

Interest = $1,000 × 0.05 × 3 = $150. Total after 3 years: $1,150.

Compound Interest

Compound interest is interest on top of interest — you earn interest on both the original amount and the interest that has already accumulated. This is how most real-world interest works (savings accounts, loans, etc.).

Total = Principal × (1 + Rate/n)^(n×t)
Where n = number of times compounded per year, t = years.

Example: $1,000 at 5% annual interest, compounded annually, for 3 years.

Year 1: $1,000 × 1.05 = $1,050
Year 2: $1,050 × 1.05 = $1,102.50
Year 3: $1,102.50 × 1.05 = $1,157.63

Compare to the $1,150 from simple interest. The difference grows bigger and bigger over time — this is the "magic of compound interest." Over 30 years at 5%, that $1,000 becomes about $4,322 with compounding vs. $2,500 with simple interest.

Mental Math Tricks: Calculate Percentages in Your Head

You do not always have a calculator handy. These tricks make it easy to estimate percentages quickly.

1. Find 10% First

10% of anything is just moving the decimal one place left. 10% of $78 is $7.80. From there, you can get most other common percentages:

  • 5% = half of 10%
  • 20% = double 10%
  • 15% = 10% + 5%
  • 25% = 10% + 10% + 5%
  • 1% = move decimal two places left (from there you can get any percentage)

2. The "Percent of" Swap Trick

x% of y is the same as y% of x. So 16% of 25 is the same as 25% of 16. Since 25% of 16 is easy (it is 4), you know 16% of 25 is also 4.

This is a huge time saver when one of the numbers is easier to work with. 8% of 50 = 50% of 8 = 4.

3. Break It Into Smaller Pieces

Need 17% of $45? Break it into 10% + 5% + 2%:

  • 10% of $45 = $4.50
  • 5% of $45 = $2.25
  • 2% of $45 = $0.90
  • Total: $4.50 + $2.25 + $0.90 = $7.65

4. Estimate, Then Adjust

For quick, rough calculations, use a nearby round number and then adjust. Need 22% of $39? Think: "20% of 40 is 8, plus a little more — about $8.50." (The exact answer is $8.58, so that is pretty close.)

For everyday use, being within 5–10% is usually good enough. If you need precision, use a calculator.

When in doubt, use a calculator. For important calculations — taxes, loans, big purchases — use a real calculator or spreadsheet. Mental math is great for quick estimates, but precision matters when money is on the line. A percentage calculator makes it fast and accurate.

FAQ: Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate a percentage of a number?

Multiply the number by the percentage expressed as a decimal. To convert a percentage to a decimal, divide by 100 (or move the decimal point two places left). For example, 15% of 200: 200 × 0.15 = 30.

How do I calculate percentage increase?

Subtract the old value from the new value to get the increase. Then divide the increase by the old value and multiply by 100. For example, from 80 to 100: (100-80) ÷ 80 × 100 = 25% increase.

How do I calculate percentage discount?

Multiply the original price by the discount percentage (as a decimal) to get the discount amount. Then subtract that from the original price. Or multiply the original price by (1 - discount percentage) to get the final price directly.

What is 20% off $50?

$10 off, so $40. 20% as a decimal is 0.20. $50 × 0.20 = $10 discount. $50 - $10 = $40 final price. Or just calculate $50 × 0.80 = $40 (since you pay 80%).

How do you calculate a 15% tip?

Multiply the bill by 0.15. Or use the quick mental math trick: find 10% (move decimal left one place), find half of that (5%), and add them together. For a $60 bill: 10% = $6, 5% = $3, total tip = $9.

How do I reverse calculate percentage?

If you know the final amount after a percentage increase or decrease and want the original number, divide by (1 + percentage) for increases or (1 - percentage) for decreases. For example: price after 8% tax is $108. Original = $108 ÷ 1.08 = $100.

Is there a difference between percentage and percentile?

Yes! A percentage is a fraction of 100 (like "I got 85% on the test"). A percentile is a ranking within a group (like "I scored in the 85th percentile" means you scored better than 85% of people). They are different concepts with similar names.

Why is compound interest so powerful?

Because you earn interest on your interest. Over time, the growth accelerates exponentially. Small amounts can grow surprisingly large given enough time. This is why starting to save early is so important — even small contributions benefit from decades of compounding.

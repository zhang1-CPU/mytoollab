Timestamp Converter: Unix Time Explained for Developers

What is that 10-digit number in every database and API response? It is a Unix timestamp, and once you understand it, you will see it everywhere. This guide explains epoch time, how to convert between timestamps and human-readable dates, and the common pitfalls that trip up even experienced developers.

What Is a Unix Timestamp?

A Unix timestamp (also called epoch time or POSIX time) is a way of representing a date and time as a single number. Specifically, it is the number of seconds that have elapsed since January 1, 1970, 00:00:00 UTC — a moment known as the Unix Epoch.

For example, at the time of writing, the current Unix timestamp is somewhere around 1.7 billion seconds. That is how many seconds have passed since New Year's Day in 1970 in the UTC timezone.

It might sound like an odd way to track time, but it is incredibly useful. Unix timestamps are the universal language of time in computing — every programming language, every database, every operating system understands them.

Quick definition. Epoch time = number of seconds since 1970-01-01 00:00:00 UTC. No time zones, no daylight saving, no calendars — just a steadily increasing number. Simple, universal, and unambiguous.

Why Is the Epoch in 1970?

The story goes back to the early days of Unix. The developers needed a starting point for their system clock, and they picked January 1, 1970, as a reasonable "beginning of time" for a new operating system. It was arbitrary — they could have picked any date — but it stuck, and now it is the standard used by essentially every computer system on Earth.

Why Timestamps Are So Useful

  • Universal. Every language, database, and system understands Unix timestamps. They are the lingua franca of time.
  • Timezone-agnostic. Timestamps are always in UTC, so there is no ambiguity about what "3 PM" means when you are working across time zones.
  • Easy to compare. Which came first? Just compare the numbers. No parsing dates, no handling different formats — just greater than or less than.
  • Easy to calculate with. Want to know the time one hour from now? Just add 3600 seconds. One day? Add 86400. Simple arithmetic.
  • Compact. Storing time as a 4-byte integer (or 8-byte for 64-bit) is much more efficient than storing a string date.
  • No daylight saving issues. Timestamps just count seconds continuously. Spring forward and fall back do not affect them.

Seconds vs. Milliseconds: Know the Difference

One of the most common sources of confusion: some systems use seconds, and some use milliseconds.

Seconds (10 digits)

Traditional Unix timestamps are in seconds. A current timestamp looks like 1717555200 — about 10 digits. This is the standard in most programming languages (Python, PHP, Ruby, C, etc.) and databases (MySQL, PostgreSQL, etc.).

Milliseconds (13 digits)

Some systems — most notably JavaScript and Java — use timestamps in milliseconds (thousandths of a second). A current millisecond timestamp looks like 1717555200000 — about 13 digits. This gives finer precision but is the same basic idea.

Microseconds and Nanoseconds

For even higher precision, some systems use microseconds (millionths of a second, 16 digits) or nanoseconds (billionths of a second, 19 digits). These are rare in everyday web development but show up in specialized systems like financial trading or scientific computing.

⚠
Common mistake. Mixing up seconds and milliseconds is the #1 timestamp bug. If your date comes out in 1970 or way in the future, check if you have the wrong unit. A quick way to tell: count the digits. 10 digits = seconds. 13 digits = milliseconds. 16 digits = microseconds.

Converting Timestamps to Dates (and Back)

While computers love timestamps, humans prefer readable dates like "June 15, 2026 2:30 PM". That is where a timestamp converter comes in — it translates between the machine format and the human format.

Using an Online Timestamp Converter

The fastest way to convert is with a dedicated tool. A good Unix timestamp converter lets you:

  • Paste a timestamp and see the human-readable date (in multiple time zones)
  • Pick a date and time and get the corresponding timestamp
  • See the current timestamp in real-time
  • Convert between seconds, milliseconds, and other units

This is especially useful when debugging — you see a timestamp in a database or API response, paste it into the converter, and immediately know what date and time it represents.

Converting in JavaScript

JavaScript uses millisecond timestamps. Here is the basics:

  • Date.now() – current timestamp in milliseconds
  • new Date(timestampMs) – convert milliseconds to Date object
  • new Date().getTime() – also gives milliseconds
  • To get seconds: Math.floor(Date.now() / 1000)

Converting in Python

  • import time; time.time() – current timestamp in seconds
  • datetime.datetime.fromtimestamp(ts) – convert seconds to datetime
  • dt.timestamp() – convert datetime to seconds

Time Zones: The Biggest Headache

Unix timestamps are always in UTC (Coordinated Universal Time, the global time standard). This is a feature, not a bug — it means timestamps are unambiguous. But it also means you have to handle time zones carefully when converting to and from local time.

UTC vs. Local Time

A single timestamp represents a single moment in time — but what the clock says depends on where you are. For example, timestamp 1717555200 is:

  • 2024-06-05 12:00:00 UTC
  • 2024-06-05 08:00:00 EDT (New York)
  • 2024-06-05 05:00:00 PDT (Los Angeles)
  • 2024-06-05 22:00:00 JST (Tokyo)
  • 2024-06-06 08:00:00 AEST (Sydney)

Same timestamp, completely different wall-clock times — and even different dates in some places! This is why storing timestamps as UTC and only converting to local time for display is so important.

Best Practices for Time Zones

  1. Store in UTC. Always store timestamps in UTC in your database. Never store local time.
  2. Convert for display. Only convert to the user's local time zone when showing the date to a human.
  3. Let the browser handle it. On the web, let JavaScript convert UTC timestamps to the user's local time using their device's time zone settings.
  4. Name things clearly. If a variable or column is in local time, name it something like created_at_local. If it is UTC, created_at_utc (or just assume everything is UTC).
  5. Use IANA time zones. When you need to specify a time zone, use standard IANA identifiers like "America/New_York" or "Asia/Tokyo" — not 3-letter codes like EST or PST (which are ambiguous).
Pro tip. Working with time zones is one of the most error-prone areas of programming. If you find yourself doing a lot of time zone math, use a library like Luxon, date-fns-tz, or Moment Timezone (for JavaScript) or pytz / zoneinfo (for Python). Do not roll your own time zone handling — you will get it wrong.

Common Timestamp Pitfalls (and How to Avoid Them)

1. The Year 2038 Problem

Remember the Y2K bug? There is a similar issue coming for 32-bit Unix timestamps. A 32-bit signed integer can only go up to 2,147,483,647 seconds — which corresponds to January 19, 2038. After that, the number will overflow and wrap around to a negative number, which computers will interpret as 1901.

Fortunately, most modern systems have already moved to 64-bit timestamps, which will be fine for billions of years. But if you are working with older systems or embedded devices, the 2038 problem is something to be aware of.

2. Off-by-One Errors with Dates

A common bug: converting a timestamp to a date and getting the wrong day. This usually happens because of time zone confusion. A timestamp that is 2024-06-05 23:00 UTC is still June 4 in New York (which is UTC-4 in summer). If you convert without specifying the time zone, you might get the wrong date.

Fix: Always be explicit about which time zone you are converting to. If you want the UTC date, make sure your conversion function uses UTC.

3. Daylight Saving Time

Daylight saving time means some days have 23 hours and some have 25 hours. If you naively add 86400 seconds (one day's worth) to a timestamp, you might not end up at the same time the next day if DST changed in between.

Fix: Use proper date/time libraries for calendar arithmetic. Do not add/subtract seconds for day/month/year operations — use the library's date manipulation functions.

4. Floating Point Precision

If you store timestamps as floating point numbers (for sub-second precision), be aware of floating point rounding errors. For most purposes, integer seconds or integer milliseconds are safer and more predictable.

5. Leap Seconds

Occasionally, a leap second is added to UTC to keep it in sync with the Earth's rotation. Unix time ignores leap seconds — each day is exactly 86400 seconds, even if a real day is slightly longer or shorter. This means Unix time is not technically a true representation of SI seconds, but for 99.99% of applications, it does not matter at all.

6. Negative Timestamps

What about dates before 1970? Those are negative timestamps. Most modern systems support them, but some older ones do not. If you need to work with historical dates, test that your system handles negative timestamps correctly.

Where You Will Encounter Timestamps

Once you know what to look for, you will see Unix timestamps everywhere:

APIs

Almost every web API uses Unix timestamps (or ISO 8601 strings, which are another standard format) for dates and times. When you see a created_at or updated_at field with a 10-digit number or an ISO string, that is a timestamp.

Databases

Most databases have timestamp or datetime columns. Some store them internally as Unix timestamps, others as calendar dates. Either way, you can usually convert to and from epoch time easily.

Log Files

Server logs, application logs, audit logs — most logging systems include timestamps for every event. Sometimes they are human-readable, sometimes they are Unix timestamps.

File Metadata

Every file on your computer has creation time, modification time, and access time — all stored as timestamps internally.

JWT Tokens

JSON Web Tokens (JWTs), commonly used for authentication, include timestamps for when the token was issued (iat) and when it expires (exp) — both as Unix timestamps.

Blockchain and Cryptocurrency

Every block in a blockchain includes a timestamp. Bitcoin and most other cryptocurrencies use Unix timestamps.

ISO 8601: The Other Standard

While we are on the topic of time formats, ISO 8601 is worth mentioning. It is a standardized string format for dates and times that looks like this: 2026-06-05T12:30:00Z.

ISO 8601 is more human-readable than a Unix timestamp but still unambiguous and machine-parseable. The "Z" at the end stands for UTC — if there is no Z, you do not know what time zone it is in, which is a problem.

Both formats have their place. Unix timestamps are great for computation and comparison. ISO 8601 is better for APIs, serialization, and anywhere human readability matters.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Unix epoch and why is it 1970?

The Unix epoch is January 1, 1970, 00:00:00 UTC — the starting point for Unix timestamps. The date was chosen somewhat arbitrarily by the early Unix developers in the late 1960s. They needed a "zero point" for their system clock and picked the beginning of the current decade as a reasonable reference. It became a de facto standard and is now used by virtually every computer system in the world.

How do I convert a Unix timestamp to a date?

The easiest way is to use an online timestamp converter — just paste the number and you instantly get the human-readable date and time. If you are programming, every language has built-in functions: in JavaScript, use new Date(timestampMs); in Python, use datetime.fromtimestamp(ts); in PHP, use date('Y-m-d H:i:s', $ts). Just remember to check if your timestamp is in seconds or milliseconds!

Is Unix time in seconds or milliseconds?

Traditional Unix time is in seconds, but some systems (notably JavaScript and Java) use milliseconds for higher precision. You can usually tell by the number of digits: 10 digits = seconds, 13 digits = milliseconds, 16 digits = microseconds. When in doubt, test it — plug the number into a converter and see if the date looks reasonable.

What timezone are Unix timestamps in?

Unix timestamps are always in UTC (Coordinated Universal Time). They are timezone-agnostic — a timestamp represents a single moment in time, and the same timestamp converts to different wall-clock times depending on your time zone. This is actually a feature: it means timestamps are unambiguous and can be compared and calculated with directly.

What is the Year 2038 problem?

The Year 2038 problem (or Y2038) is a potential bug in systems that use 32-bit signed integers for Unix timestamps. The maximum value of a 32-bit signed int is 2,147,483,647, which corresponds to January 19, 2038. After that, the number will overflow and wrap to negative, representing dates in 1901. Most modern systems have already switched to 64-bit timestamps, which are fine for billions of years.
